Redesign

This UX research & UI rework started with a realization, that app’s current UI restricts me from translating efficiently, especially using one hand.

However, I couldn’t make a redesign just to meet my expactations.

To find out if discovered issues were universally applicable across users, I’ve thoroughly analyzed app’s market nichce, target audience and problems that the product solves.

UX Issues

    I analyzed my interactions and usage patterns within app and formulated following UX issues:

  • Position of "Enter text" and language switcher is far away, which makes it hard to access with one hand on big screen devices.

  • Can't pick one word while translating a sentence, which forces user to copy a word, cancel translation, paste word back.

  • No organization of translations makes it hard to find and group translations.

  • Lack of Material Design 2.0 update makes UI feel outdated, especially compared against other Google apps.

I asked myself - are these objective problems?
Are there more users affected by these issues?

Secondary research

As a starting point, I choose to go through app reviews & feedbacks in order to find mentions of similar issues directly from users.

I started by scanning through Play Store & App Store to tackle feedback from active userbase and moved on Twitter, where I was lucky to get professional opinion from UX/UI lead designer at Todoist.

Afterwards, I referred to UX & academic articles which helped me to understand current trends and industry standards.

I’ve also found similar redesign casestudies, which helped me to narrow down my approach and avoid numerous pitfalls.

However, the most beneficial pieces of information came directly from Google's reports, researches & design updates.

I was lucky to get insights about app’s positioning on the market directly from Google’s intern and UX Lead.

By examining new features in updated web-version which aren’t present in the app I derived global user demand discovered by Google.

Most of the gathered information supported my claims and helped me to deeper understand the problems users were facing.

In order to adress researched issues and be able to test them in real life setup, I needed audience to work with.

I used customer development methodology in order to define and research end user.

Customer Research

Based on gathered insights, I outlined different problem statements and surveyed Google Translate users to formulate the one I’ll be focusing on:

As
[ an active user of multiple foreign languages ],

I want
[ to save my translations in sophisticated order by grouping together thematically similar words and phrases ],

so that
[ I can effortlessly find past translation ].

Using this defininition, I refined userbase for my UI proposals.

To find the most relevant features, I iteratevely sketched, wireframed, prototyped and presented my ideas to these users for feedback.

Many iterations later, gathered feedback showed that instead of reworking existing functionality, I should focus on cherry-picking new features by expanding UI capabilities.

UI Redesign

To achieve conistency in design for both Android & iOS, I followed Material Design 2.0 and recreated custom Google Material Theme by analyzing avaliable Google apps.

That was needed in order to retain Google’s brand identity and create a crossplatform unified design.

Moving main input element to the bottom sheet component and adding fast navigation between recent languages on long tap simplified one-hand use.

Instead of copying word, deleting sentence and pasting word back, it is now possible to translate a word from sentence by directly taping on it.

During customer research, I found out that significant part of Android users didn’t know about “phrasebok” submenu for starred words.

Thus, I’ve converted submenu into a filter for starred words - together with language pairs filter, it simplified search and grouping of past translations.

Google has reportedly tested flashcards functionality in the app - if the future research proves that there is a demand for language learning through vocabulary building, grouping words by phrasebooks could be a vital part of it.

That notion goes in hand with my research as I've discovered a demand for additional grouping of favorite translations.

I’ve added a new phrasebooks section - these work as folders for favorite translations of the same source or theme.

Don't take my word for granted, try all of these features in my MVP prototype.
